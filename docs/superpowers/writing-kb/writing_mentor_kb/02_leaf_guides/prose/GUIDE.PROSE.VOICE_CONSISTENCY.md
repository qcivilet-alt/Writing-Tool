# Voice Consistency

A craft guide for understanding, identifying, and maintaining your writing voice.

---

## What Voice IS

Voice is the distinctive pattern of choices a writer makes at the sentence level — not a single feature but a fingerprint composed of many. It includes:

- **Sentence length tendency** — Do you favor short declarative sentences, long flowing ones, or deliberate variation?
- **Formality register** — Conversational with contractions, or elevated and precise?
- **Diction range** — Plain, mid-register, ornate, or a mix that shifts with purpose?
- **Rhythm pattern** — Staccato, flowing, cadenced, or unpredictably varied?
- **Characteristic constructions** — Fragments, parentheticals, lists, rhetorical questions?

**Voice is NOT the same as:**
- **Style** — style is broader and includes structural choices (scene length, POV, tense)
- **Tone** — tone is the emotional register of a specific passage and can shift within a consistent voice
- **Register** — register is the formality level, which is one component of voice

---

## Common Voice Drift Patterns

### AI Editing Homogenization
The most common source of voice drift in contemporary writing. AI editing tools smooth prose toward statistical averages — removing productive quirks, evening out sentence rhythm, replacing specific diction with generic alternatives. The result reads as "correct" but sounds like nobody.

### Revision Smoothing
Each revision pass tends to sand down edges. After three or four passes, the writer's distinctive patterns may have been polished away. This happens with or without AI tools, but AI accelerates it.

### Style Guide Over-correction
Applying rules mechanically (eliminate passive voice, cut adverbs, shorten sentences) without judgment. Style rules are guidelines that serve clarity — applied without taste, they flatten voice into compliance.

### POV Contamination
In multi-POV fiction, one character's voice bleeding into another's. Each POV character should have distinct sentence patterns, vocabulary range, and thought rhythms.

## Identifying Your Own Voice

The Voice Profile concept captures your baseline characteristics so drift can be detected:

1. **Gather samples** — 3-5 passages you consider representative of your best writing
2. **Identify patterns** — What sentence lengths dominate? What constructions recur? What's your default formality?
3. **Name your preserves** — 5-7 features that make your writing yours (these should never be "fixed")
4. **Name your weaknesses** — 2-3 habits that genuinely weaken your prose (these are fair game for editing)

The distinction between "preserve" and "weakness" is the heart of voice-aware editing. A writer who uses fragments for emphasis is making a choice; a writer who uses fragments because they can't construct complex sentences has a weakness.

---

## When Inconsistency is a Problem vs. Intentional

**Problem:** Voice shifts without narrative justification — the writer sounds different in chapter 5 than chapter 2 for no discernible reason. Often caused by writing across long time spans, incorporating AI suggestions unevenly, or mid-project style changes.

**Intentional:** Voice modulates to serve the story — a character's narration tightens during high-tension scenes, loosens during reflection. Register shifts when POV shifts. Formality drops in dialogue-heavy passages.

The test: can you articulate *why* the voice shifted? If yes, it's modulation. If no, it's drift.
