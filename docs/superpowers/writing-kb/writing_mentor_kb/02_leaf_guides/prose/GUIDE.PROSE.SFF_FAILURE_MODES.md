# SFF Prose Failure Modes

The 13 most common prose-level failure modes in speculative fiction, with diagnostic signs and editorial actions. Each failure mode is a category — the editorial action describes the direction of the fix, not a specific textual correction.

Route this guide when the genre is speculative fiction and the symptom involves SFF-specific prose issues.

---

## 1. Infodumping / Expository Lumps
**Diagnostic sign:** Paragraphs of worldbuilding with no character action or emotional engagement. The narrative stops while the author explains.
**Editorial action:** Break exposition into fragments; attach each fragment to a character action, decision, or reaction. Information lands when a character needs it, not when the author wants to deliver it.

## 2. "As You Know, Bob" Dialogue
**Diagnostic sign:** Characters telling each other things they already know. "As you know, our kingdom has been at war for three years."
**Editorial action:** Remove the information from dialogue entirely. Find a different delivery vehicle — internal thought, environmental detail, or dramatic situation that makes the information necessary.

## 3. Head-Hopping / POV Violations
**Diagnostic sign:** Reporting thoughts, feelings, or internal states of non-POV characters. "She didn't know that he was planning to betray her."
**Editorial action:** Identify the single POV character per scene. Remove all information the POV character cannot perceive. Convey other characters' states through observable behavior.

## 4. Purple Prose
**Diagnostic sign:** Ornate language that is decorative rather than meaningful. Description that calls attention to the writer's vocabulary rather than serving the scene.
**Editorial action:** Apply Le Guin's Chastity test: does each descriptive element serve the story (mood, characterization, foreshadowing, worldbuilding)? If it only serves to be pretty, cut it.

## 5. Filter Words / Psychic Distance Errors
**Diagnostic sign:** Unnecessary mediation between character and experience. "He felt his toe catch on the root" instead of "His toe caught on the root."
**Editorial action:** Search for filter verbs (felt, noticed, saw, heard, realized, thought, wondered, seemed). Remove the filter and state the experience directly. This deepens psychic distance to Level 4 (direct experience).

## 6. Inconsistent Register
**Diagnostic sign:** Modern slang mixed with archaic language without character justification. A medieval-coded world where characters say "okay" and "definitely."
**Editorial action:** Define the register per POV character. Audit dialogue and internal narration against that register. Consistency matters more than historical accuracy.

## 7. White Room Syndrome
**Diagnostic sign:** Dialogue or action occurring in an ungrounded space. Characters talk and move but there's no sense of where they are physically.
**Editorial action:** Ground each scene opening in concrete sensory detail. Use Sanderson's Pyramid of Abstraction: open with 4 specific details, narrow to 3, then 2, then 1 as the scene focuses.

## 8. Said Bookisms
**Diagnostic sign:** Creative dialogue tags replacing "said" — exclaimed, opined, queried, retorted, proclaimed.
**Editorial action:** Replace with "said" or action beats. "Said" is invisible to readers; creative tags call attention to themselves and away from the dialogue.

## 9. Weak Verbs / Passive Voice in Action
**Diagnostic sign:** Passive constructions in high-tension scenes. "The sword was drawn" instead of "She drew the sword." Action sequences that feel sluggish.
**Editorial action:** Convert to active voice in action scenes. Reserve passive for deliberate effect (obscuring agency, emphasizing the object). Check verb specificity: replace "moved" with the precise verb.

## 10. Front-Loaded Exposition
**Diagnostic sign:** Opening chapters dominated by backstory, worldbuilding, and context before any character is onstage with a problem.
**Editorial action:** Get characters onstage first — acting, wanting, conflicting. Weave backstory through character experience. The reader earns context by caring about the character first.

## 11. Faux-Archaic Dialogue
**Diagnostic sign:** Inconsistent "thee/thou" usage, stilted grandiloquent speech that no human would produce, or mixing archaic forms with modern syntax.
**Editorial action:** Remove archaic forms unless consistent, purposeful, and character-specific. If the world is not literally medieval England, archaic pronouns are a costume, not a necessity.

## 12. Pacing Collapse Through Over-Crowding
**Diagnostic sign:** Every scene exhaustively described at the same level of detail. No differentiation between critical turning points and connective tissue.
**Editorial action:** Apply Le Guin's "leaping" principle: identify the scene's trajectory, then cut everything not on that trajectory. Major scenes get full treatment; minor scenes can be summarized or skipped.

## 13. Subgenre Style Mismatch
**Diagnostic sign:** Prose conventions from one SFF subgenre applied to another. Epic-fantasy exposition density in a sword-and-sorcery story. Grimdark cynicism in mythic fantasy. Progression-fantasy mechanical detail in literary crossover.
**Editorial action:** Audit prose against target subgenre conventions. Each subgenre has different expectations for exposition density, sentence complexity, pacing, and tonal register.

---

## Using This Guide

These failure modes describe categories. Real manuscripts often exhibit multiple modes simultaneously (infodumping + filter words + inconsistent register is a common cluster). When revising:

1. Identify which failure modes are present
2. Prioritize by impact on the reader's experience
3. Address structural failures (infodumping, front-loaded exposition) before surface-level ones (said bookisms, filter words)

For systematic detection, use prose-editor with the `full-review` concern tag and speculative fiction genre calibration.
