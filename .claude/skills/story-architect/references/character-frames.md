# Character Frames — Cross-Genre Reference

Reference document for story-architect character commitment gate.
Maps the fiction character frame (want/need/misbelief/arc) to memoir,
creative nonfiction, and long-form essay equivalents.

---

## 1. Narrative Fiction Character Frame

Backed by `GUIDE.CHARACTER.WANT_NEED_MISBELIEF.md`.

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Want** | What the protagonist actively pursues at the story's start — a specific, concrete goal. Not a feeling, not an abstraction. |
| **Need** | What the protagonist actually requires but doesn't yet know they need — often in tension with the want. |
| **Misbelief** | The false belief the protagonist holds that organizes their behavior and prevents them from getting what they need. Must have once been genuinely seductive or useful. |
| **Arc** | The trajectory of internal change: where the protagonist begins, how the story's events force confrontation with the misbelief, where they end up internally. |

## 3. Creative Nonfiction Character Frame

| Concept | Fiction Analogue | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **The Obsession** | Want | The specific, driving compulsion that sent the writer toward this subject — not "I was curious" but "I could not stop thinking about this." |
| **The Hidden Claim** | Need | The argument the material forces, often different from the stated thesis — what the piece must say to be true. |
| **The Reporter's Complicity** | Misbelief | The assumption or bias the writer brought that the material must expose — what they wanted to be true, and what reality disrupted. |
| **The Form as Argument** | Arc | How structure and form enact the piece's meaning — the reader's journey, not the writer's change. |

---

## 4. Long-Form Essay Character Frame

| Concept | Fiction Analogue | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **The Animating Question** | Want | The specific, genuinely open question the essay attempts — not a topic, not a thesis, but a real inquiry the writer does not yet have the answer to. |
| **The Persona** | Need | The constructed version of the self the essay performs — the facet of intelligence this inquiry requires the essayist to become. |
| **The Governing Contradiction** | Misbelief | The paradox or irresolvable tension the essay holds in suspension — unlike fiction's misbelief, this may remain unresolved at the end. This is genre-normative, not a gap. |
| **The Movement of Mind** | Arc | The traceable cognitive journey of the essayist's thinking — where they start, how they pursue, where they arrive or deliberately fail to arrive. |

---

## 5. Cross-Genre Structural Note

Fiction misbelief is overcome. Memoir's lie is exposed. CNF's complicity is
interrogated. Essay's governing contradiction may be held permanently — this
is genre-defining, not a design gap.

Gate questions for the misbelief-equivalent must account for this: do NOT
treat an unresolved governing contradiction as a gap in essay genre.

---

## 6. Minimum-Viable Resolution Criteria Per Frame Field

All frame fields across all genres must meet these thresholds to pass gap scan:

- **Clause threshold:** Field must contain at least one subject + verb
  construction. Single words or noun phrases fail. ("revenge" fails;
  "the protagonist wants revenge against her father" passes)
- **Specificity threshold:** Proper nouns, concrete objects, or named
  relationships must appear at least once per major character field.
- **Internal completeness:** Certain fields require paired sub-fields
  (e.g., want + obstacle, wound + consequence).

### Genre-specific adaptation

- Essay's "Governing Contradiction" is exempt from resolution-as-closure —
  an articulated paradox passes even if unresolved.
- Memoir's "Earned Distance" requires temporal markers (before/after) to
  demonstrate the narrator's shifted perspective.
